PLAYGROUND

Research | Branding | Wireframes | Prototype


At the Playground, you can find the perfect lipstick shade for your skintone. Each shade includes undertone details making it easy for anyone to color match.


VIEW PROTOTYPE







PROBLEM

Browsing makeup can be fun but frustrating when you can’t find a shade that matches your unique skintone.

SOLUTION

The Playground offers more diverse shades with skintone details included, so you can easily find your lipstick.













RESEARCH

To better understand the makeup user, I conduted a survey to learn more about the shopping habits of makeup consumers. With 50 respondants, below are the main findings:



90% Choose lipsticks as their favorite products
60% Stated too many products to decide from
85% Prefer Formula over brand or price
75% Stated products don't match their skintone
90% Shop at Sephora in store or online
90% Purchase new products monthly

    BRANDING

    When creating the Playground identity, it was important to incorporate lipstick formula and the shopping habits of users. When brainstorming, I decided to use my own liptstick products to help inspire potential logos. The term “swatch” and “play” stood out as they conveyed a fun and common way to explore makeup. Users will swatch lipsticks to see if it matches their skintone and play with different shades. Thus, the name Playground resulted as the MVP for this design.







    Using the swatches I created, lipstick images and the name Playground, I sketched the final logo below based on user feedback. Users understood the product right away with a lipstick icon, however users also enjoyed the swatch icon to emphasize formula. I created a combo of the two logos as the final visual part of the brand identity.

    USER TESTING

    I conducted various preference and navigation tests to ensure simple page flows that make sense to the user. Each test was disributed via email, social media, and other online forums as well as in person interviews. With a focus on customization, check out, sign up and writing a review, below are the results of some user tests:






    80% of users voted for Option B, using images to choose their lipstick finish as oppose to buttons. Having a visual idea of what the lipstick finish will look like helps the user create a more personalized lipstick.









    When adding a lipstick to their shopping cart/bag, users were asked if they prefer to be notified on the top right vs. above the Add To Bag button. Although 55% choose option A, I found the responses were not adaquete enough or I wanted to know more information. I did a secondary user test to find out if having two “Add to Bag” buttons would work better for the user, with a notification above the button regardless. This prevents the user from scrolling back up and they can add items in various places, creating a better shopping experience




    Sign Up & Checkout Feedback:

    • Users liked less pushes to sign up
    • After signing up, no clear indication of sign up
    • Users preferred sign-up process at checkout or after purchase

    Write Review Feedback:

    • Users requested reviewer's name on reviews
    • Users preferred recommendations with reviews
    • 'Write a Review' modal window instead of a seperate page





    What I learned from these user tests was how to implement changes I wasn't expecting such as less sign up pushes or the shopping cart highlighted in multiple places. The results of these user tests helped reduce pain points and engage users more in the shade selection process.
